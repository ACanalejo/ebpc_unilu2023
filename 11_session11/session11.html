The issue evolution model

The issue evolution model

Carmines & Stimson (1986)


→ It tries to answer a fundamental question about political change: why some new issues become politicized?


Importantly, the model moves the focus from the transformations occurring in society to the agency of political parties

The issue evolution model

Figure 1 from Carmines & Stimson (1986)

Shifting elite positions

The main thesis is that political parties politicize new issues to increase their support


Politicization = salience X polarization


What issues do they choose to politicize?

What new issues do party politicize?


To become politicized, new issues must be emotionally charged


→ That is, voters need to have strong emotions about them (i.e., latent conflict, identity-related)


Thus, when parties take clear positions (i.e., clarity), voters’ emotions toward the issue is reflected toward the parties (i.e., affect)

Ultimately, if the new issue become sufficiently important, it should bring mass alignment

The issue entrepreneurship model

The issue entrepreneurship model

De Vries & Hobolt (2012; 2020)


→ It adapts the issue evolution model to the European multi-party systems


Again, the model focuses on the agency of political parties, but it focuses on the role of new political parties: why some new parties become successful?

The issue entrepreneurship model


Distinction between three party types:

  1. Mainstream governing parties

  2. Mainstream opposition parties

  3. Challenger parties

The issue entrepreneurship model

In contrast to the issue evolution model, mainstream opposition parties do not have incentives to politicize new issues in multi party systems


→ The potential costs may be larger than the expected benefits (e.g., cartel party thesis)


Therefore, new issue politicization is brought by challenger parties

→ They talk of new issue dimensions

Why challenger parties?


  • They are the losers of the dominant dimension of competition

  • They do not have coalitional records

  • Therefore, their expected benefits are higher than the potential costs

→ What issues? Those more dividing (ie., emotionally charged) and not aligned along the dominant dimension

Limitations to the issue-entrepreneurship model

Response papers

“Just because elites are at the beginning of the process (they make the issue salient first, according to the authors) does not mean that they are contributing to the creation of a burning issue more than other actors like parties and mass opinion.” (Apolline Bouly)


“For this theory to work completely, we need the voters to be more into”issue voting” rather than party identification. In fact, maybe the issue voters would also constitute a volatile electorate, since they would be voting according to issues that had beed raised and not for the party itself” (Apolline Bouly)

Response papers

“Policy-seeking aims need not imply electoral benefit, as, especially in multi-party systems, some challenger parties may simply be ideologically oriented groups who hold priorities other than vote-gaining.” (Liam O’Brien)


“Parties can become issue entrepreneurs for a specific cause or to protest a major crisis or scandal.” (Liam O’Brien)

Response papers

“The free choice of issues for parties that appear to be political losers does not convince me in a general sense. Most (if not all parties) have a certain ideological position. While it is easier to change the party’s position if it is “young”, I think that even for political losers there is a tendency of “freezing” ideological orientation.” (Paul-Vincent Heisenberg)


“And the role of the media (mass media or even social media) should also be highlighted, as they have a crucial role in shaping opinion.” (Irina Wais)
